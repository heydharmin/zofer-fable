The argument

How the agents cross-examined each other

Every finding was handed to an adversarial critic told to refute it, ground the verdict in a real case study, and score it. Findings didn't reach the redesign on vibes — they survived, got narrowed, or died. The most interesting is where two lenses reach opposite conclusions about the same pixel.

◆ Lenses disagree Should the '+' button be labelled? Two agents, opposite verdicts.
Usability critic“Overreach — you bundled three controls, two already conventionally labelled. The '+' sits beside 'Add your car' text; the mode dropdown shows 'Personal Chauffeur' with a chevron, the standard iOS pull-down. Only the promo ↗ is a genuine ambiguity.” → survives, reduced · ICE 270
Accessibility“Proximity doesn't help VoiceOver. The '+' exposes no programmatic accessible name — the screen-reader user just hears ‘button’. The fix is essentially free and lands on the two primary CTAs.” → survives · ICE 486 · ranked #1
ReconciledBoth kept. The synthesis merges them: a visible word for sighted users and an accessibilityLabel for AT. Same button, two layers — neither critic was wrong.
Case studies citedNN/g Icon Usability (icon+adjacent-text is acceptable) · WebAIM Million 2024 (empty buttons on ~28% of home pages) · WCAG 4.1.2 Name, Role, Value.
✕ Killed “Demote 'Add your car' below the booking path.” Conversion · rejected 4·3·8
Critic“The finding misreads the business model. This isn't an Uber ‘book any driver’ app — the mode is Personal Chauffeur, and the service mix (Garage Pick & Drop, RTA Inspection, Monthly) is car-owner-centric. The chauffeur likely drives the user's own car — so ‘Add your car’ is a legitimate first action, and demoting it could break the primary flow.”
OutcomeConfidence collapses to 3/10. The demote proposal dies; only the residual ‘label the +’ nugget survives — and that gets folded into the #1 accessibility fix above. The one finding an agent talked the council out of.
Case study citedNN/g Icon Usability — refutes the finding: the '+' is contextually labelled by the card's own copy, so the demote rests on a misread of what the app does.
✓ Survives “The two featured cards hide their price.” 3 lenses raised it · ICE 343
Critic“Pay-Per-Minute & Hourly are genuinely variable — a single number could understate the total or look scary. And price-hiding can be a deliberate luxury tactic.”
Rebuttal“Survives. The ‘from AED’ framing is the standard hedge for variable pricing, and the inconsistency argument is independent: the app already prices its errand services, so silence on the headline products reads as evasive, not premium — especially when the category leader Careem shows estimates.”
Case study citedBaymard Institute — “extra cost too high” is the #1 checkout-abandonment reason (~48%). Uber/Careem both show an upfront per-tier estimate under surge — an established, expected pattern.
◑ Narrowed “Carousel has no scroll cue and opens mid-sequence.” Usability · trimmed, kept
Critic“Half-wrong. A partial next-card peek is the recommended swipe affordance, not an accidental clip — so ‘no cue exists’ is false, and ‘always start on card 1’ is a preference.”
Kept core“Two things remain objectively broken regardless of the peek debate: the heading truncated mid-word (‘Chauffeu’), and the carousel opening deep in the sequence with a half-rendered title. Drop ‘start on card 1’; keep the overflow fix.”
Case study citedBaymard — a partial sliver is the strongest swipeability cue; pagination dots alone are too subtle. NN/g — carousels are low-engagement overall (don't over-invest).
◑ Reframed “The 'Where to?' bar competes with the service cards.” Conversion · ICE 210
Critic“The search-bar + mode-selector + tiles layout is the canonical super-app pattern (Careem, Uber) — users are habituated to it, so ‘competes’ is refuted.”
Reframed“What survives is narrower: in Careem/Uber the flagship product is a destination ride, so ‘Where to?’ matches. Here the flagships are time-based, so the same prompt carries false information scent. Fix the copy, not the layout.”
Case study citedPirolli & Card information-foraging (scent) — entry labels mismatched to their destinations reduce task completion.
◑ Weakened “Selecting a mode produces no visible state change.” Usability · ICE 150 · lowest survivor
Critic“You can't know that from a static screenshot — you never saw the interaction, so the ‘no feedback’ premise is unverifiable. And Uber/Careem ship both entries fine.”
Residual“The defensible core: the selected mode isn't echoed where booking begins. Real, but low-severity polish — scored down, not out.”
Case study citedNN/g Heuristic #1, Visibility of System Status.

MOVE A

Name every hand

Explicit accessible names on all three glyph controls; a visible word on the two that matter (+ Add, See plans ↗); promote the mode selector to a labelled combobox.

→ resolves #1 + the CTA half of #4
MOVE B

Price the browse

‘from AED’ anchors on both featured cards and Monthly, reusing the proven blue badge. For the meter: pair the rate with a plain estimate and one honest control line (‘Paused in traffic’).

→ resolves #5, #6
MOVE C

Prove it's safe, show a face

One verifiable trust line at the top (4.9★ · 12,400 trips · RTA-licensed) and real driver imagery. One line, not a wall — Baymard: 1–3 signals beat zero; 7+ backfire.

→ resolves #2
MOVE D

Fix the broken hero

Constrain the promo text to the safe area so it wraps instead of clipping; test against Dynamic Type. Keep the peek affordance; don't open mid-sequence with a half-rendered title.

→ resolves #3 + the layout half of #4
Sequencing (from the council): #1, #3, #4 are all Small effort and share the promo/labelling surface — ship them as one fast pass (highest ICE, lowest cost). #2, #5, #6 are the medium-effort trust-and-price layer that moves the booking-start metric; #5 and #6 share the featured-card component, so build them together.
